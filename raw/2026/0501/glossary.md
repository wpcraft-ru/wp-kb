# Glossary

One of the first challenges you might face when setting up a site is understanding the terminology. There's a lot of specialized jargon, which can get confusing quickly! This glossary is intended to clarify the terms we use to discuss websites.

For more in-depth explanations of some of the technical terms and concepts, please see [Tech Jargon Explanations](https://wordpress.com/support/tech-stuff-explanations/).

## Terms

- **Address** (web address, URL) — The reference for a specific resource on the World Wide Web, such as a website. Also known as a URL (Uniform Resource Locator).

- **Automattic** — The company that owns and creates WordPress.com, as well as several other web platforms and services.

- **Block** — A distinct structural element on a page, post, or widget area. In the WordPress Editor, blocks allow you to add and edit specific content areas, such as text, images, and videos.

- **Browser** (web browser) — A software application used to view and interact with sites on the World Wide Web. Common browsers include Chrome, Edge, Firefox, and Safari.

- **Cache** — A stored record of information. Commonly refers to browser cache (temporarily storing sites you've visited) or site cache (data stored on servers to deliver content faster).

- **Canonical URL** — The default version of your site's address preferred by search engines such as Google or Bing.

- **Content** — Anything published on your site: text, images, videos, audio files, and any other information made available to visitors.

- **Dashboard** — The main control panel area for your WordPress.com site. Two versions exist: the standard WordPress.com dashboard and the "classic" WP Admin dashboard.

- **DNS Record** — A setting that determines how your domain works. Modifying DNS records allows you to connect your domain to third-party services.

- **Domain Name** (domain) — An address people use to visit your site.

- **Domain Connection** — The association of a specific address to your site so that typing that address into a browser loads your site.

- **Domain Registration** — The lease of a specific address by an individual or entity.

- **Drop-down Menu** (sub-menu) — Shows additional items when you hover or click on an item in your menu.

- **Editor** — The interface that enables you to edit content on your site. WordPress.com has two editors: the WordPress Editor (block editor) and the Classic Editor.

- **Footer** — Displays content in widget areas at the bottom of your site, below the main content of every page or post.

- **Full Site Editing (FSE)** — A collection of features enabling you to edit your entire site using blocks.

- **Header** — Displays content at the top of your site, above the main content. Commonly contains site title, tagline, and navigation menus.

- **Home Page** (homepage, front page) — The first page visitors see when they visit your site.

- **Jetpack** — A plugin built into plugin-enabled WordPress.com sites. Includes site activity records, backups, optimized performance, anti-spam, security monitoring, and more.

- **Landing Page** — A standalone page generally used for a single purpose (call to action), such as encouraging signups or purchases.

- **Legacy Feature** — A feature that has been retired from development. May still be accessible but is no longer being improved.

- **Menu** — A list of links for accessing your site's content, typically displayed at the top of your site.

- **Page** — A static document for displaying content on your site. Common pages include Home, About, and Contact.

- **Plan** — A packaged set of options that add functionality, storage space, and other features to your WordPress.com site.

- **Plugin** — A piece of software used to add extra features to WordPress. Some are made by Automattic (Jetpack, WooCommerce), others by third-party developers.

- **Plugin-Enabled Site** — A site that has activated full hosting features available on all WordPress.com paid plans.

- **Post** — A document for displaying dynamic content on your site, generally listed in reverse chronological order. Posts are always associated with a date.

- **Registrar** — A company or organization which handles the registration of domain names with ICANN.

- **SEO** (Search Engine Optimization) — The process of improving your site to help it rank high in search engine results.

- **Sidebar** — A column to the left or right side of a site's main content area, used to display menus, widgets, and other content.

- **Site** (website) — The collection of pages, posts, and other content published on the World Wide Web.

- **Subscription** — Can refer to the dated period of an upgrade plan, or to site visitors signing up for email updates when you publish new posts.

- **Template** — Controls the layout of your pages or posts. Modifying a template affects the look and feel of individual or groups of pages/posts.

- **Theme** — Controls the visual design of your site, including layout, color scheme, fonts, and where logo/menus/widgets appear.

- **Third-Party** — Refers to products (plugins, themes) which aren't made or developed by WordPress.com.

- **Upgrade** — A product sold by WordPress.com to add functionality to your site, such as plan subscriptions and domain registrations.

- **URL** (Uniform Resource Locator) — Also known as an address, the reference for a specific resource on the World Wide Web.

- **Widget** — A modular piece of content that can be added to specific areas of your site, such as the sidebar or footer.

- **WooCommerce** — A plugin that enables you to create a store to sell physical and/or digital products.

- **WordPress** — A free, open-source software system for publishing content on the World Wide Web.

- **WordPress.com** — A platform that lets you build a website using the WordPress software, with managed hosting.

- **WordPress.org** — The repository site for the WordPress open-source software.

- **World Wide Web** (WWW) — An interconnected system of documents and other informational resources accessible on the Internet.

- **WP Admin** — The "classic" WordPress dashboard, accessible by adding /wp-admin/ to the end of your site address.
